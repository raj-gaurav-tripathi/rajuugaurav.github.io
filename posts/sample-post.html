Where Do Cosmic Magnetic Fields Come From?

Every galaxy we've ever measured is threaded with a magnetic field, coherent over tens of thousands of light-years. Where did they come from — and why does that question end up pointing at dark matter?

Point a radio telescope at almost any galaxy and you'll find a magnetic field: microgauss-strength, ordered over scales far larger than any single star or supernova remnant could plausibly organize on its own. Galaxy clusters have them too, and there are hints of magnetic fields even in the emptiest voids between galaxies. Magnetism, it turns out, is not a local curiosity — it is a cosmic default.

The trouble with dynamos

The standard explanation for how galactic fields reach their observed strength is the dynamo effect: turbulence and differential rotation in a galaxy's gas can take a weak magnetic field and amplify it over billions of years, in much the same way Earth's core sustains its own field. Dynamo theory works well as an amplifier. What it cannot do is start from nothing — it needs a "seed" field already in place before it can get to work.

So the real question just moves one step back: where did the seed come from? Astrophysical processes in the first stars and galaxies — the Biermann battery, misaligned pressure and density gradients in ionized gas — can produce something, but the numbers are uncomfortably small and uncertain. That gap is what "magnetogenesis" research is trying to close: finding a mechanism that produces a seed field early enough, and strong enough, for the dynamo to take over.

A particle physics origin

One family of proposals looks past astrophysics entirely and asks whether fundamental physics could have generated a seed field directly. This is the angle I find most interesting: ultralight dark matter, or an axion-like particle, behaves like a classical field that oscillates coherently once the universe has expanded enough. If that field couples to electromagnetism through a gauge-kinetic interaction, its oscillation can pump energy into specific electromagnetic field modes through a resonance effect — amplifying a microscopic quantum fluctuation into a macroscopic seed field, no dynamo required to get started.

What makes this appealing isn't just that it works on paper — it is that the same coupling constant governing the resonance also controls other observables, so the scenario is falsifiable in more than one place at once.

That's the part I find genuinely exciting about working on this problem: it sits at a real intersection. Getting the mechanism right means taking dark matter model-building seriously as particle physics, while the payoff — if the numbers work out — is a testable, observable imprint on the universe we can already see. I'll write more here as the technical side of this project develops.
