§ About

A bit more about my path so far

I'm currently in the final stretch of the BS-MS dual-degree programme in Physics (with a minor in Mathematics) at IISER Kolkata, and actively applying to PhD programmes in theoretical and observational cosmology.

My research, under Prof. Koushik Dutta, sits at the intersection of dark matter phenomenology and early-universe cosmology. I'm especially drawn to problems where a well-understood field-theory mechanism — a coupling, a resonance, an instability — leaves a concrete, in-principle-observable imprint on the universe we see today. Magnetogenesis from ultralight dark matter is exactly that kind of problem: coupling a light scalar to electromagnetism through a gauge-kinetic term drives certain momentum modes of the gauge field through tachyonic resonance, amplifying them exponentially and seeding the fields that astrophysical dynamos later grow into the magnetic fields measured in galaxies and clusters today.

Outside this core project, coursework in Space Astronomy and Topology has kept me close to both the observational side of astrophysics and the mathematical machinery that theoretical cosmology leans on heavily. Longer term, my interests span theoretical physics more broadly — cosmology, quantum field theory, and particle physics — and where dark matter model-building connects them.

§ Research

Magnetogenesis from ultralight dark matter

Where do the magnetic fields threading galaxies and galaxy clusters come from? My research explores one candidate answer: that they trace back to the oscillation of an ultralight dark matter field — or an axion-like particle — in the early universe, coupled to electromagnetism through a gauge-kinetic term.

How it works, in four steps

1

The dark matter field oscillates

An ultralight scalar — dark matter, or an axion-like particle — begins coherently oscillating around the minimum of its potential once the Hubble rate drops below its mass.

2

It couples to the electromagnetic field

A gauge-kinetic coupling links the oscillating scalar to the photon field, turning its time-dependence into a time-dependent effective mass in the gauge field's mode equations.

3

Certain modes hit tachyonic resonance

Floquet analysis of the resulting periodic mode equation reveals narrow bands of momentum where the effective mass-squared briefly turns negative, driving exponential growth within that band.

4

The amplified field seeds cosmic magnetism

The exponentially amplified gauge-field modes become the seed magnetic fields that astrophysical dynamos later amplify into the fields observed in galaxies and clusters today.
